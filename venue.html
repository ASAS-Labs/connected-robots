Venue: The Grove School of Engineering & The City College of New York

Beyond its academic prestige, the Grove School of Engineering (GSoE) and its parent institution, The City College of New York (CCNY), are steeped in unique traditions and historical “firsts”—and sit at the heart of Manhattan’s “Harvard-on-the-Hudson” story.

United Nations University at CCNY: The UNU Hub for Remote-Sensing & Sustainable Innovations for Resilient Urban Systems (R-SIRUS) is the first United Nations University research and training hub of its kind in the United States, connecting global sustainability science with New York City—a natural partner for EARS-CONN’s focus on Digital-Earth and connected cities.

The “Harvard of the Proletariat”

A democratic experiment

Founded in 1847 as the Free Academy, CCNY was the first free public institution of higher education in the United States. Its mission was to give the children of immigrants and the working class access to a Harvard-level education based on merit—earning nicknames like “Harvard of the Proletariat” and “Harvard-on-the-Hudson.”

Nobel Prize powerhouse

CCNY alumni and faculty have earned 10 Nobel Prizes—a record that leads all public colleges in the country.

Social mobility leader

CCNY is consistently ranked #1 by the Chronicle of Higher Education for its overall mobility index: it is more likely than almost any other school to move a student up two or more income quintiles.

Grove School & Steinman Hall

The bridge-builder’s legacy

The engineering school is housed in Steinman Hall, named for alumnus David B. Steinman (Class of 1906). Steinman was a legendary civil engineer who designed more than 400 bridges, including the Mackinac Bridge in Michigan and the Henry Hudson Bridge in New York City.

A nuclear past

In 1956, two faculty members who had worked on the Manhattan Project helped bring a nuclear reactor to the college. It was originally sited in the stands of Lewisohn Stadium and supported the nuclear engineering program until 1972.

Student grit & glory

Students regularly excel in major regional competitions—for example, the CCNY Concrete Canoe team has placed first in the Metropolitan Conference, proving that even heavy materials can float with enough engineering.

Campus traditions & quirks

Good-luck rituals

On the historic North Campus, students often rub Abraham Lincoln’s nose (on a bust in Shepard Hall) or George Washington’s shoe (on a statue) for good luck before exams.

Architectural guardians

The original 1906 neo-Gothic buildings are adorned with some 600 unique grotesques—gargoyle-like carvings representing academic disciplines, including a stone figure holding a tiny model of Shepard Hall.

Alcove debates

From the 1930s through the 1950s, the cafeteria in Shepard Hall was famous for its “alcoves,” where students held fierce, nonstop political debates. Alcove 1 was legendary as a place where Trotskyists and Stalinists argued from morning till night.

Famous “firsts” at CCNY

  • Student government: CCNY claims the nation’s first student government, established in 1867.
  • Inclusive fraternities: In 1899, Delta Sigma Phi was founded at CCNY as the first national fraternity to accept members regardless of religion or race.
  • Sports history: CCNY remains the only team ever to win both the NCAA and NIT men’s basketball tournaments in the same year (1950). The college also hosted the first intercollegiate lacrosse game in the U.S. in 1877.
