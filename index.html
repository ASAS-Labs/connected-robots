Why EARS-CONN

Digital-Earth treats the planet as a shared, evolving model: wide-area sensing from space meets street-level truth from robots, vehicles, and IoT. Together they give planners and communities a picture that stays current—not a static globe, but infrastructure for decisions.

Ethical embodied-AI is how that model stays usable and accountable: learning and acting with governance, safety, and fairness—not scale alone.

Climate stress, aging infrastructure, and rapid AI adoption make urban assurance a large, shared problem: no single field owns it. EARS-CONN is deliberately transdisciplinary, so engineers, earth scientists, insurers, planners, and policymakers debate sensing, models, and ethics in one program rather than in parallel silos.

EARS-CONN brings researchers, builders, insurers, and city stakeholders together to connect those layers for smarter, more resilient cities. Roots of the idea reach back to the 1990s Digital Earth vision and early NASA globe visualizations; today’s digital twins and embodied sensing extend that path. Further reading: NASA SVS, The Digital Earth (1999); ASME on digital twins for Earth.

Session overview

The program takes a transdisciplinary view of a system-scale challenge: linking science, engineering, finance, and governance so evidence moves from sensors to decisions without losing accountability. One thread across talks and panels: embodied-AI plus sensing infrastructure supplies the data and policy hooks for a living Digital-Earth: urban twins that support ethical, measurable sustainability assurance.

Industry Speakers

Dr. Mata Khalili

Robotics Research Scientist, Nokia Bell Labs

Dr. Mata Khalili is a Robotics Research Scientist at Nokia Bell Labs, where she works at the intersection of wireless communications, robotics, and sustainable network design. Her research focuses on how next-generation connectivity—particularly 6G—can be engineered to deliver genuine value for users, vertical industries, and society at large, while remaining environmentally, socially, and economically sustainable.

Dr. Khalili's work spans energy-saving mechanisms for mobile networks, goal-oriented communications, and the role of 6G as an enabler for sustainability across industries. She is a strong advocate for designing 6G infrastructure with a low energy footprint and reduced CO₂ emissions as first-order constraints, not afterthoughts. Her broader research agenda addresses impact assessment and life cycle analysis of mobile networks, as well as network optimization strategies for energy sobriety.

Value and sustainability should guide the "why" and the "how" of 6G. The 6G technology should be designed to enable added-value services to its users and the society. Among these services, sustainability-enabling services for society and vertical industries are of particular interest. In addition, it is mandatory that the 6G infrastructure and services are themselves sustainable from the environmental, social, and economic perspectives — with environmental sustainability involving particularly low energy consumption and low CO₂ emissions.

This talk addresses how researchers and industry can collaborate on the following themes:

  • Energy saving mechanisms for mobile networks: sleep mode, energy-efficient physical layer, and network optimization for energy sobriety
  • Goal-oriented communications and sustainability
  • Impact assessment and life cycle analysis of mobile networks
  • 6G networks as enablers for sustainability of society and industries
